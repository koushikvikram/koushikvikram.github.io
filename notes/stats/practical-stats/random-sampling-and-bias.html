Random Sampling and Bias

Population refers to a large, defined data set. It may sometimes refer to a theoretical or imaginary set of data. Sample refers to a subset of the population.

Random Sampling is a process in which each available member of the population has an equal chance of being chosen for the sample at each draw. A simple random sample is a sample created through the process of random sampling. Sampling can be done
  • with replacement - observations are put back in the population after each draw for possible future reselection
  • without replacement - observations once selected are unavailable for future draws.
Data quality often matters more than data quantity when making an etimate or a model based on a sample.

Properties of quality data in Data Science involves
  • completeness
  • consistency of format
  • cleanliness
  • accuracy of individual data points
A representative sample is one that accurately represents, reflects, or “is like” your population. A representative sample should be an unbiased reflection of what the population is like.

Sample bias means the sample was different from the population it was meant to represent in some meaningful nonrandom way. This meaningful difference can be expected to continue occuring for other samples drawn in the same way as the first.

Self-selection bias is a bias that is introduced into a research project when participants choose whether or not to participate in the project, and the group that chooses to participate is not equivalent (in terms of the research criteria) to the group that opts out.

Statistical bias refers to systematic errors produced by the measurement or sampling process.

Errors can occur due to
  • random chance - from an unbiased process, occurs at random, does not tend strongly in any direction
  • bias - due to random error + bias in process
A biased result is often an indicator that a statistical or machine learning model has been misspecified or an important variable has been left out.

The key to random sampling is the proper definition of an accessible population.

Stratified sampling - population is divided into strata and random samples are taken from each stratum.

In the era of big data, sampling helps in
  • reducing bias
  • allowing greater attention to data exploration and data quality


Big data may be needed instead of just samples when the data is not only big, but also sparse.

The sample mean $\bar{x}$ is "observed" whereas the population mean $\mu$ is often "inferred" from smaller samples.
