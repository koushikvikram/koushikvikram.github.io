Selection Bias

If you specify a hypothesis and conduct a well-designed experiment to test your hypothesis, you can have high confidence in your conclusion.

However, this is often not the case. We usually perform analysis on large datasets that are already available.

Data Snooping is the process of extensive hunting through the data in search of something interesting.

Selection bias refers to the practice of selectively choosing data (consciously or unconsciously) in a way that leads to a conculsion that is misleading or ephemeral (lasting for a short time).

Typical forms of selection bias include
  • vast search effect
  • nonramdom sampling (sampling bias)
  • cherry picking data
  • selection of time intervals that accentuate a particular statistical effect
  • stopping an experiment when the results look "interesting"
Vast search effect - If you repeatedly run different models and ask different questions with a large data set, you are bound to find something interesting. Is the result you found truly something interesting or is it the chance outlier?

Using one or more holdout set(s) to validate performance and target shuffling are techniques that can be used to guard against the vast search effect.

Target shuffling is used to estimate the baseline performance of a predictive model. It is particularly useful for identifying false positives, or when two events or variables occurring together are perceived to have a cause-and-effect relationship, as opposed to a coincidental one. The more variables you have, the easier it becomes to ‘oversearch’ and identify (false) patterns among them—called the ‘vast search effect’. It's expected that the quality of a model (accuracy, area under the curve, R², etc.) will decrease drastically if the target values were shuffled as any relationship between input and target was removed. It's advisable to repeat this process (target shuffling + model building + model evaluation) many times and record the bogus result in order to receive good estimates on how well the real model performs in comparison to randomized data.

Regression to the mean - successive measurements on a given variable show that extreme observations tend to be followed by more central ones. So, attaching special focus and meaning to the extreme value can lead to a form of selection bias.
