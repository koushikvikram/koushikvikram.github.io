Calculus Without Graphs

Previous:   Calculus By Hand & Calculus Not By Hand

So far, we've relied heavily on graphs and tables to calculate derivatives. In this section, let's use our knowledge of secants and slopes to calculate derivatives from equations.

First example

Let's revisit our previous where speed is a function of time, represented by the equation \(s=t^2\)



We've seen that the derivative of 's' with respect to 't' (i.e. the rate of change of 's' with 't') is denoted as \(ds \over dt\) and it is the slope of 's' when plotted against 't'. To calculate the slope, we take a small triangle along the line and divide the triangle's height by it's extent. In short, $${ds \over dt} = {height \over extent}$$

We consider two points that are at a distance of \(\Delta t\) on either side of t:
  • \(t-\Delta t\)
  • \(t+\Delta t\)

The difference between these points is the extent.
Therefore, $$extent = (t+\Delta t) - (t-\Delta t) = 2\Delta t$$

The height is the difference between the values of 's' at \(t+\Delta t\) and \(t-\Delta t\). i.e. \((t+\Delta t)^2 - (t-\Delta t)^2\). Simplifying the expression, we get: $${ds \over dt} = {(t+\Delta t)^2 - (t-\Delta t)^2 \over 2\Delta t}$$ $$= {t^2 + \Delta t^2 + 2t\Delta t - t^2 - \Delta t^2 + 2t\Delta t \over 2\Delta t}$$ $$= {4t\Delta t \over 2\Delta t}$$
Thus, $${ds \over dt}= 2t$$ We can verify the correctness of this equation by plugging in different values of 't' and checking it against the table in the previous article.
